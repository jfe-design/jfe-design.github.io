VMLY&R - Federal Government

ADF Careers Digital Transformation

Co-led the UX overhaul of the Australian Defence Force recruitment platform. Uncovered that ~40% of recruits were changing their role of interest mid-pipeline - a signal that misaligned expectations were driving post-enlistment attrition. Built the internal case for a personalisation feature stream and consolidated a 200+ page, four-domain architecture into a coherent 100-page single domain.

20% uplift in application completion rates. Client proceeded with personalisation features beyond initial scope.
UX Research Information Architecture Stakeholder Management Prototyping Data Analysis
Explore this project
The Problem

The ADF Careers platform processed hundreds of thousands of visitors but nobody had modelled what was actually happening once candidates entered the pipeline. When I mapped the data, a pattern emerged: roughly 40% of candidates were changing their role of interest partway through the process. That isn't just a UX problem - it means the platform was generating enrollees who had already self-selected into a mismatched role, creating a post-enlistment attrition problem that was expensive and almost invisible from the outside.

The Approach

I built the internal case for a personalisation feature stream, worked with a core team of five inside a wider multidisciplinary team of around 12, and ran two sprint workshops to scope and stage-gate the solution. The feature set included zero-party data surfacing at entry, a three-role comparison tool, and progressive content complexity tied to pipeline stage - so a candidate just browsing saw different content to one who had committed to assessment. In parallel, I restructured the information architecture, consolidating a 200+ page, four-domain site into a coherent 100-page single domain. The infrastructure change made the experience change possible.

The Result

Application completion rates increased by 20%. The client proceeded with the personalisation features beyond the original scope - which in consulting terms means the work held up under scrutiny and the team trusted the rationale behind it.

The 40% role-change signal was the catalyst for the entire personalisation stream
Consolidating four siloed architectures into one coherent domain was the foundation for personalisation
AssuranceLab - Global Fintech

AI-Powered Internal Support Tool

Identified a 12-hour communication lag across AU, EMEA, and US time zones creating a compounding risk for a consulting team expected to double its client portfolio. Built a business case that secured founder backing, packaged the context bundle, and iterated the AI tool from a closed founder pilot through to full team deployment.

Eliminated response delays. Supported team capacity to more than double client workload, and lifted confidence and competence for new joiners.
AI Tool Design Service Design Business Case User Testing
Explore this project
The Problem

AssuranceLab was a fast-growing cybersecurity compliance consultancy operating across AU, EMEA, and the US without any off-hours support mechanism. When a junior consultant in Australia needed to answer a client question at 9am, the senior who knew the answer was asleep in New York. That 12-hour dead zone was a structural problem, not a staffing one - and it was about to get worse, because the firm was planning to double its client load.

The Approach

I identified the pattern, quantified the risk, and wrote a business case that got founder backing. Then I built it: I packaged the context bundle myself - SOC2 evidence frameworks, client-facing response patterns, tool logic for Vanta and Drata - and iterated the AI tool through a closed pilot with the founder before expanding it. It wasn't designed as a replacement for expertise; it was designed to give new consultants a confident first answer at 2am so senior staff could focus on work that actually required their judgment.

The Result

Response delays were eliminated. The team's capacity to handle client work more than doubled without a proportional increase in headcount. It became a staff development tool in its own right: new joiners built competence faster, and the team carried its workload with more confidence as the company pursued its acquisition targets.

The gap between AU close-of-day and US start-of-day left the team with no expert cover for roughly half the clock
Each stage was validated before expanding - the tool earned its rollout rather than being mandated
Agency Xi - NSW Dept of Education

EDConnect Ways of Working Playbook

Embedded within EDConnect's shared services directorate to raise design maturity across 2,200 school communities. Spent 37 hours in the field across six locations, synthesising 500+ data points from 69 stakeholders into a 'Ways of Working' playbook - 29 HCD tools, four personas, a service blueprint, and five strategic plays.

HCD capability built across 880 employees. Playbook adopted organisation-wide.
Service Design Qualitative Research Workshop Facilitation Change Management
Explore this project
The Problem

The NSW Department of Education's EDConnect directorate served 2,200 school communities. The people who worked there were intelligent and committed - and fundamentally stuck. Not because of a lack of effort, but because every team had its own way of working with no shared language for collaboration, problem surfacing, or improvement. Nobody had mapped it. Nobody had asked the staff what they actually needed.

The Approach

I spent 37 hours in the field across six locations, talking to 69 stakeholders and synthesising more than 500 data points. That research shaped everything: four personas representing the real range of people in the directorate, a service blueprint of the actual current state, and a Ways of Working playbook containing 29 HCD tools and five strategic plays. The 'Walk in My Shoes' train-the-trainer programme was designed to make the capability self-sustaining - we weren't just teaching people tools, we were giving them the confidence to keep using them after we left the engagement.

The Result

HCD capability was embedded across 880 employees. The playbook was adopted organisation-wide - not just in the team we'd been working with. That kind of spread is the real measure of whether work like this actually lands.

The research scale was non-negotiable - you can't design a shared language for an organisation without first understanding how fragmented it really is
The train-the-trainer layer was the difference between a deliverable that got filed and one that changed how 880 people worked

The Problem

The NSW Registry had a digital transformation on the horizon - but no one would approve the spend without understanding what was actually being transformed. The service had been running for decades without ever being fully documented end-to-end. My job was to produce the baseline: a current-state view rigorous enough to make the case for what came next.

The Approach

I ran stakeholder interviews across the key touchpoints in the birth certificate journey - collecting data on what people did, where they got stuck, and what they wanted the service to do instead. What the research surfaced was a structural pattern of siloed communication: teams holding information that other teams needed, with no reliable mechanism for sharing it. I synthesised the findings into a current-state blueprint flagging validated pain points and framing the opportunities a future project could address.

The Result

The blueprint gave the organisation the confidence to proceed, and became part of the case for the broader transformation programme that followed.

Teams were holding information others needed - the citizen experience was the downstream consequence of internal fragmentation
The blueprint was the first time the organisation had seen its own service mapped end-to-end - that visibility alone changed the conversation
LocalPeople - Dept of Health

Aged Health UX Evaluation & Design

Contracted to establish a compliance baseline for digital tools used across aged health service pathways - tools that had never been assessed against federal government accessibility and usability policy. Designed a structured heuristic evaluation programme, trained junior designers to run it, and synthesised findings into a prioritised remediation roadmap.

Mentoring programme directly contributed to the agency securing additional project scope.
Heuristic Analysis Accessibility Mentorship Service Mapping
Explore this project
The Problem

A suite of digital tools used across aged health service pathways - registration, referral, case management - had never been assessed against federal government accessibility and usability policy. Nobody knew how non-compliant they were, or how much effort it would take to fix them. You can't scope a remediation without a baseline, and nobody had one.

The Approach

I designed a structured heuristic evaluation programme and used it as a development opportunity for junior designers embedded on the client side - walking them through the evaluation framework, then tasking them with systematically auditing each tool across the affected service areas. What came back was a prioritised list of failures ranked by severity and frequency of use. I synthesised that into a remediation roadmap that gave the client a clear picture of what needed fixing first and why.

The Result

The agency secured additional project scope directly off the back of the mentoring programme - the client saw that the team's capability had lifted, not just that the audit had been completed. That's what happens when you structure junior work as a genuine learning experience rather than a delegation exercise.

The severity/frequency matrix determined the remediation sequence - highest-traffic, most-broken flows came first
Teaching junior designers to run the audit wasn't a shortcut - it was deliberate capability-building that changed what the client thought the agency could do
Independent Project

Buyers Agent Property Search Tool

Designed and built an end-to-end property intelligence terminal for a private buyers agent launching his own brand. Scoped requirements through user interviews, designed the interface, and developed a functional proof of concept application with Firebase Studio - integrating Google Maps API for proximity data and combining property search, CRM, and meeting scheduling into a single iPad tool.

Functional POC delivered in under a month. Demonstrates end-to-end capability from user problem to working prototype.
Firebase Google Maps API Product Design CRM Design
Explore this project
The Problem

A private buyers agent launching his own brand was spending the majority of his working week on tasks that weren't his core skill: scraping property data from multiple sources, cleaning and comparing it, maintaining client records across spreadsheets, and managing his own calendar. By the time he got to a client meeting - the part of his work that actually differentiated him - he was time-poor and under-prepared.

The Approach

I started with user interviews. We mapped exactly where his time was going and what decisions he was making manually that could be supported with better tooling. The brief that emerged was specific: a single terminal on his iPad combining a property search and listing tool (built around his cached property set with Google Maps proximity data), a CRM for clients and vendors, and a planning and scheduling layer with AI-assisted meeting prep and admin automation. Using Firebase Studio, I evolved the interface into a proof of concept prototype.

The Result

A functional proof-of-concept delivered in under a month. More importantly, it demonstrates something the rest of this portfolio doesn't: the ability to move from a user problem all the way to a working prototype without handing off to a development team.

User interviews mapped where every hour was going - the problem wasn't motivation, it was tooling
Three tools, one interface - the brief was to eliminate context-switching, not just digitise existing workflows
About

A non-linear path
to a coherent point of view.

My career has not followed a straight line - and I think that is the most useful thing about me. I started in industrial and product design, moved into experience design at agencies, spent time in law enforcement, worked in cybersecurity compliance, and have consistently found my way back to the same thing: designing systems that work better for the people using them.

That range gives me something most designers don't have: comfort in environments where the stakes are real and the brief is genuinely ambiguous. I've presented design decisions to military officers, built business cases from scratch to convince fintech founders, and conducted user research in contexts where the people I was interviewing had never been asked what they thought before.

I am not interested in design as decoration. I am interested in design as the mechanism through which complex systems become usable - and I am most useful on the kind of engagements where that distinction matters.

Currently based in Sydney.
